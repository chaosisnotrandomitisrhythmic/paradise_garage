## Context

Juan Aguiñaga (a.k.a. **OG Juan**) runs **Tombolo**, a Madrid avant-garde electronic party + label founded summer 2018, and previously ran **Limbo Club** (Thursday residency at Sala Siroco, 2013–2018). At the time of this interview Tombolo was about 18 months old and the label didn't exist yet (launched 2021). Bookings around that era trended toward house, breakbeat, electro, jungle — guests have included Soichi Terada, DJ Assault, DJ Fett Burger, Marie Davidson, Shanti Celeste, Jayda G, Egyptian Lover, DJ Dustin, LTJ Bukem, Ebo Taylor.

The show "Mezclas Relativas" is structured as introspective Q&A: each guest answers the same set of questions while choosing the music that scores their narrative. This episode covers two of those question-blocks before tape ends.

## I. Huella e Identidad — how personality translates into work

**The honest opener:** Juan describes himself as having "bastantes demonios" in the territory where personality meets work. Emotional, perfectionist, obsessive — gets attached to his projects and never lets the work feel "enough." The pattern: never says no, takes on everything, decides "puedo con todo" and pushes forward — but suffers privately under the load.

He contrasts himself with people he reads as steady — his cousin Carlos ("el tío más pancho del universo") and his brother, both of whom seem to have an ordered life. His own head, by his own description: a kind of tornado with furniture flying around inside it. The shield he puts up: **fake it till you make it**. He's not pretending to do things — he genuinely executes — but the cost is that other things get dropped along the way, and he keeps half-expecting something he didn't control will eventually catch up with him.

**On what he wants people to take away:** that they have a good time. He says it almost apologetically ("muy cheerful, pero vamos") but lands on it as the through-line: a packed room of strangers in one box, watching one person do something — that's the whole point. Some of his projects are "selfies" he does for himself; the music side is for the audience. If nobody comes, it doesn't mean anything.

**The self-perception bit (most candid section):** Asked how he thinks others see him, he names the vanity directly. Throwing a party has a vain component — you're not the star, you're not the one playing, but without you it doesn't happen. He likes being seen as someone who's active, energetic, who pulls a lot of things off. When people come up after a set and tell him he killed it, he plays it down ("no, hombre, no, nada") but knows in private that he likes it.

**On collaboration:** two-headed answer. Wants to go solo, hates being constrained, has clear ideas he wants to execute his way — but is also insecure enough that he needs someone to validate his judgment, which keeps pulling him into teams. The friction: he's more of an executor than the people he ends up partnering with, and the same people whose validation he wants don't always want to commit at his pace. Still, he recognizes that going pure-solo would be smaller — other people's opinions and influences feed the work.

## II. Archivo, revisión y reciclaje — personal archive practices

**Self-diagnosis:** "un auténtico desastre a nivel organización." Generates massive amounts of archive — photos, music, records — and has no system for any of it.

- **Photos:** Just started a folder ironically named *Random Adventure*, pulling images off all his external drives, mixing family trips with night-out stuff, no consistent labeling — works backwards from memory ("2015, when I met Chico Blanco" — "no, that was 2019").
- **Music:** Got fed up one day, **wiped his entire iTunes library**. Now has ~100 GB sitting in one undifferentiated folder, can't find anything. Joke: he's waiting for a hypothetical future kid to organize it for him.
- **Vinyl:** ~600 records, all out of order. Confesses to **giving records away mid-set on impulse** — someone at a party asks what a track is, he hands them the record. Same energy at a wedding: someone complimented his tie, he gave it to them.
- **Booking list:** **doesn't keep one.** Tried once, never looked at it again. Writes to artists ad hoc; if six respond at the same time he panics about flaking on the ones he can't take. Doesn't write down ideas at all.
- **Journal:** wants to start one — both an emotional diary and a daily-events log — to be able to look back in 15 years at "this rollercoaster emocional" of a year. Says he can write well when he sits with it; what actually exists right now are scraps written in moments of rage or sadness, lost in the chaos, sometimes rediscovered six months later and shocking to re-read.

**Counter-point from the host:** there's a cost to over-organizing too — too much time tidying, not enough time doing. He agrees: the balance is the thing.

## Why this matters for Paradise Garage

A Madrid scene primary source on what running parties looks like from the inside — the temperament, the operational chaos, the relationship between vanity and craft. Tombolo's playbook (year 5 in 2019: weekly residency → label-less party brand → eventually a label) is one of the closer reference architectures to what this project might grow into. His honesty about the cost of executor-mode is also useful prophylaxis against romanticizing it.
